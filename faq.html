Basics

What is NandaIndex?+

NandaIndex is a federated index of AI Catalog-formatted resolution records, a global switchboard for agent discovery. Each entry maps an identity to a discovery entry point. Rather than acting as a centralized registry, NandaIndex is a distributed index of pointers: authoritative discovery remains with each system, multiple resolution paths can coexist, and federation enables cross-boundary discovery.

Is NandaIndex a registry?+

No. Rather than acting as a centralized registry, NandaIndex is a distributed index of pointers. Authoritative discovery remains with each system; an entry resolves an identity to the correct discovery entry point, whether that is an AI Catalog, a DNS-based system, a gateway, a registry, or an agent card.

Do I need NandaIndex if I already publish AI Catalog?+

Not for direct resolution. When an enterprise publishes an AI Catalog at a well-known endpoint and controls the domain, direct resolution already works, and NandaIndex is not in the critical path. No extra infrastructure is needed; any requester can fetch the catalog directly. A NandaIndex entry is optional, useful as a fallback or federation entry point.

What is a discovery object?+

The discovery entry point an identity resolves to, whether that is an AI Catalog, a DNS-AID record, a gateway, a platform registry, or a hosted agent card.

Entries and resolution

What does an entry contain?+

Each resolution entry is an AI Catalog-compatible record that acts as a typed pointer, indicating the discovery mechanism (for example DNS-AID, catalog, or gateway), the location or access method, and the metadata required for interpretation and trust. In the index-record format this is an identifier and displayName, a mediaType, a url or inline data, a description and tags, the publisher, and metadata.

When does an entry use a URL versus inline data?+

AI Catalog provides a consistent representation for both direct resources, such as agent cards and MCP descriptors, and indirect discovery paths, such as catalogs, DNS, and registries. A url points to a directly fetchable target; inline data carries a discovery path such as a DNS-AID lookup (organizationDiscoveryName and agentDiscoveryName).

What is a resolution role?+

In the index-record examples, the metadata field org.projectnanda.resolutionRole carries values such as nested-ai-catalog, dns-aid-pointer, smb-agent-card, personal-agent-card, and ard-endpoint.

How does resolution proceed?+

Resolution separates two concerns: resolution determines where and how discovery should begin, and discovery finds capabilities via mechanisms such as ARD. The flow is Identity → Resolution → Discovery → Invocation: a requester starts with an identity, queries a resolution system, receives AI Catalog-formatted resolution entries, follows the discovery path each specifies (ARD, DNS lookup, gateway), and then proceeds to verification and invocation.

Identities and deployment

What identities are supported?+

An identity can be a domain, an email, or a URN. The paper's examples use domain identities (urn:ai:domain:...) and email identities (urn:ai:email:...); the Browse catalog also demonstrates system identities (urn:ai:system:...) for discovery mechanisms. All are carried in the same record format.

What is permissionless deployment?+

The runtime, the agent card, and the domain can each live with a different provider. An SMB's agent may be hosted on AWS while its agent card is hosted by a third party such as host39.org; an individual who does not own a domain name can run a personal agent on AWS, Azure, GCP, or another provider while the agent card lives elsewhere. NandaIndex resolves the identity regardless.

Can an SMB or individual register without a domain?+

Yes. SMBs typically rely on hosted infrastructure and may not operate catalogs, DNS discovery, or gateways; for them NandaIndex becomes the primary discovery entry point. Individuals typically lack domain ownership and discovery infrastructure and rely on identity-based resolution: an email identity maps directly to a hosted agent card.

What is host39.org?+

A third-party agent-card host. It lets SMBs and individuals publish agent-card information even when they do not own a DNS domain or cannot run catalog infrastructure on their own domain. The runtime and brand domain can live with other providers; host39.org serves the card, and a NandaIndex entry points to it.

Ecosystem

How does NandaIndex relate to AI Catalog?+

NandaIndex reuses AI Catalog as a unified record format for resolution. No new schema is introduced; AI Catalog becomes the bridge across heterogeneous systems. NandaIndex strengthens AI Catalog by making it the preferred framework through which other discovery systems can be bridged.

How does it relate to AGNTCY ADS and ARD?+

ADS and NandaIndex operate at different layers. ADS provides the federated discovery infrastructure, implementing AI Catalog as the representation format and ARD as the standard query interface; NandaIndex provides the resolution layer that directs traffic to systems like ADS. Together they form a complete path: Identity → NandaIndex → ADS (ARD) → AI Catalog → Agent.

Does it replace DNS or DNS-AID?+

No. For a DNS-based discovery path, NandaIndex acts as a bridge, not a replacement, and discovery continues through DNS-AID. The entry carries inline routing data (organizationDiscoveryName and agentDiscoveryName) so the DNS-AID path is reachable from the switchboard, while the domain's DNS stays authoritative.

How do emerging registries (telecom, IoT, sovereign) join?+

Each publishes a resolution entry that points to its native mechanism. A resolution entry may point to platform or vertical registries, among other targets. The resolution layer does not replace these systems, it connects them, allowing heterogeneous discovery mechanisms to coexist while remaining interoperable, without requiring convergence on a single approach.
